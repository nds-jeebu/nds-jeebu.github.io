Policy Iteration:

Suppose that instead of starting off with some arbitrary value function V0V^0V0, we would like to start of with an arbitrary policy, π0\pi^0π0. From this policy we would like to determine the optimal policy π∗\pi^*π∗.

Claim: There is an iterative procedure that we can use to construct π∗\pi^*π∗ starting with an arbitrary π0\pi^0π0.

Derivation (Policy Improvement):

Recall that we are currently working with deterministic policies. Furthermore, let’s restrict ourselves to greedy policies.

Start by taking π0∈Πdet\pi^0 \in \Pi_{det}π0∈Πdet​

We have can construct this policy’s associated value function: Vπ0 V^{\pi^0}Vπ0

Define (Policy Improvement):

A policy π\piπ improves upon policy π0\pi^0π0 when for all s∈Ss \in Ss∈S: Vπ(s)≥Vπ0(s)V^{\pi}(s) \geq V^{\pi^0}(s) Vπ(s)≥Vπ0(s)

Define (Greedy Policy Relationship):

When a policy π∈Πdet\pi \in \Pi_{det}π∈Πdet​ is greedy with respect to a value function VVV we have: TπV=T∗V T^{\pi}V=T^*VTπV=T∗V
All that’s happening here is that the policy π\piπ is taking the actions that maximize V(s)V(s)V(s) for all s∈Ss \in Ss∈S. This is exactly what the Bellman Optimality Operator does!

Claim: A policy π\piπ that is greedy with respect to Vπ0V^{\pi^0}Vπ0, improves upon π0\pi^0π0.

  • First, observe that: Tπ0Vπ0=Vπ0T^{\pi^0}V^{\pi^0}=V^{\pi^0}Tπ0Vπ0=Vπ0
  • Furthermore, it should be clear that the Bellman Optimality Operator induces greater or equal value than Tπ0T^{\pi^0}Tπ0: Vπ0=Tπ0Vπ0≤T∗Vπ0 V^{\pi^0}=T^{\pi^0}V^{\pi^0} \leq T^*V^{\pi^0}Vπ0=Tπ0Vπ0≤T∗Vπ0
  • Recall that the policy π\piπ is greedy with respect to Vπ0V^{\pi^0}Vπ0, therefore: Vπ0=Tπ0Vπ0≤T∗Vπ0=TπVπ0 V^{\pi^0}=T^{\pi^0}V^{\pi^0} \leq T^*V^{\pi^0}=T^{\pi}V^{\pi^0}Vπ0=Tπ0Vπ0≤T∗Vπ0=TπVπ0
  • If we apply TπT^{\pi}Tπ to both sides we get:Vπ0≤TπVπ0≤TπTπVπ0 V^{\pi^0} \leq T^{\pi}V^{\pi^0}\leq T^{\pi}T^{\pi}V^{\pi^0}Vπ0≤TπVπ0≤TπTπVπ0
  • Keep doing this nnn times and we get:Vπ0≤(Tπ)nVπ0 V^{\pi^0} \leq ({T^{\pi}})^n V^{\pi^0}Vπ0≤(Tπ)nVπ0
  • In the limit as n→∞n \rightarrow \infinn→∞, we get that (Tπ)nVπ0=Vπ({T^{\pi}})^n V^{\pi^0}=V^{\pi}(Tπ)nVπ0=Vπ by Banach’s Fixed Point Theorem! Therefore, we get the desired result: Vπ0≤VπV^{\pi^0} \leq V^{\pi}Vπ0≤Vπ
    Note: It’s pretty simple to show that if T∗Vπ0=Vπ0T^*V^{\pi^0}=V^{\pi^0}T∗Vπ0=Vπ0, then π0\pi^0π0 is an optimal policy. If you want to rigorously show this, you just recall that T∗T^*T∗ is a contraction on a complete, normed space.

Observe: Policy improvement yields an algorithm for computing the policies closer and closer to the optimal policy π∗\pi*π∗, this is called Policy Iteration.

Convergence:

Policy iteration iteration converges in a finite amount of steps given the kinds of MDPs we are working with. Furthermore, as you notice that we are using the Bellman Operator many times in policy improvement, the running time will be a function of some desired error! (Look to Post 4 for details.)

Comparison:

Question: What are the differences between the two approaches?
Answer:

  1. Recall the quality function, Qπ(s,a)Q^{\pi}(s,a)Qπ(s,a). The way we extract a policy currently is: πA(a∣s)=argmaxaQπ(s,a)\pi_A(a|s)=argmax_aQ^{\pi}(s,a)πA​(a∣s)=argmaxa​Qπ(s,a)

  2. The argmaxargmaxargmax operation doesn’t care how much larger values are as long as they are the largest! For example: argmax([.01,.99])=1argmax([.01,.99])=1argmax([.01,.99])=1 and argmax([.49,.51])=1argmax([.49,.51])=1argmax([.49,.51])=1.

  3. Applying this reasoning to our situation, it should be clear that we can come up with an optimal policy before coming up with an optimal value function.

  4. In practice, Policy Iteration can converge to a solution in fewer iterations than Value Iteration.

  5. In practice one iteration of Policy Iteration is more expensive to run than one iteration of Value Iteration.

So, depending on your MDP, it may be beneficial to explore one or the other. Note that they both will eventually converge to an approximate solution given all of the assumptions we have made so far!

Let’s Question Assumptions:

Okay, let’s be real. Are MDPs with finite action and state spaces really interesting? Furthermore, are deterministic, stationary policies actually a good assumption to make about agents? Will the environment be so cut and clear? Meaning, will the transition function, reward function and states all be known apriori?

Let’s address these three big sets of assumptions:

Finite State & Action Spaces:

This assumption works out for a lot of games! So, yes, there are some interesting applications. However, you can imagine by looking at the algorithms that these can be quite slow given that the spaces get bigger and bigger. Also, there are a whole lot of problems where the state and action spaces are infinite. When we encounter these, this approach will simply not work! Why? Well, we no longer have a complete normed space! We can’t guarantee that limits converge within the space. Furthermore, computation time would be unrealistic.

Deterministic, Stationary Policies:

I’ll be brief. As humans, our policies change with time. So, if we want to solve interesting problems involving changing objectives, we should be looking at the literature to see what’s next!

Knowns and Unknowns:

Alright, here’s the point where I should be clear to mention a very important assumption that is absolutely just not going to fly. We can’t expect to know the transition probability. Imagine you are an agent in crossing the street, there is nondeterminism all around you. Finally, we cannot know the rewards. For example, think of what the rewards for an autonomous vehicle would be. It’s actually this problem (of learning rewards) that I will be investigating in my future posts!
